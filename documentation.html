The Challenge

The digital intake assessment used by the activity department was lengthy and poorly aligned with how staff actually learned about residents.

What Was Happening

  • Incomplete entries
  • 30-45 min per assessment
  • Poor data quality
  • Staff frustration
  • Missed opportunities

Core Issue

Assessment designed based on what administrators thought staff needed, not how staff actually gathered information or what they needed for activity planning.

Phase 1: Observe & Discover

Duration: Week 1-2 | Methods: Direct observation, contextual inquiry

What I Did

  • Observed 8 staff members conducting intake assessments
  • Shadowed during natural conversations with residents
  • Watched when staff abandoned digital tool for paper notes
  • Tracked completion times (25-50 minutes)

Key Observations

Staff learned most through casual conversation during activities, meals, and hallway encounters - NOT during formal assessment
Digital form had 47 questions across 6 screens with significant scrolling
Natural conversation flow was disrupted by stopping to type

Critical Discovery

Staff naturally gathered information in 3 key moments:

1 Initial Hello (5 min) - Basic preferences, mood, communication style
2 During First Activity (15 min) - Observing engagement, energy, social comfort
3 Follow-up Conversation (10 min) - Deeper interests, past experiences, goals

But the current tool forced everything into one 45-minute formal interview

Phase 3: Synthesize Insights

Duration: Week 3 | Methods: Affinity mapping, journey mapping, pain point analysis

Essential (Must Know)

  • Communication preferences
  • Mobility/physical needs
  • Cognitive status
  • Social comfort level
  • 3-5 activities enjoyed
  • Things that bring joy

Redundant/Irrelevant

  • Medical diagnoses (in chart)
  • Medication list (in chart)
  • Admission date (in system)
  • Detailed work history
  • Childhood activities
  • Food preferences (dietary)
Key Pattern: Staff needed just enough information to invite someone to their first activity - not a complete life story

Phase 4: Ideate & Co-Design

Duration: Week 3-4 | Methods: Brainstorming workshops, paper prototyping

Workshop Output

Staff co-designed a 3-part intake process:

1 Quick Connect (5 min) - Bedside or common area
2 Activity Observation (10 min) - During/after first activity
3 Preference Deep Dive (15 min) - Follow-up after rapport built

Key Innovations

  • Split into multiple brief touchpoints instead of one long session
  • Checkbox lists with examples instead of open text fields
  • Visual activity menu to prompt resident memory
  • Mobile-friendly format for completion during natural encounters

Key Design Learnings

1. Design WITH Users, Not FOR Users
Co-designing with activity staff ensured the tool matched real workflow, not imagined workflow
2. Observe Natural Behavior Before Asking Questions
Watching how staff actually gathered information revealed the mismatch between natural process and forced formal assessment
3. Less Can Be More
Cutting 25 questions made the assessment more effective, not less. Shorter tool got completed more often with better quality
4. Timing Matters as Much as Content
Splitting into 3 brief touchpoints instead of one long interview aligned with natural relationship building
5. Prototypes Reveal What Words Can't
Physical testing revealed specific pain points that interviews couldn't surface
16 / 23

Challenges Overcome

Challenge 1: Resistance to Change

Problem: Staff skeptical about "another redesign"

Solution: Started with pure listening, no solutions. Co-designed together.

Result: 100% staff buy-in

Challenge 2: IT Limitations

Problem: IT said mobile and auto-save "couldn't be done"

Solution: Proved concept value with paper prototype first

Result: IT found workaround

Challenge 3: "Missing Information"

Problem: Admin worried about cutting questions

Solution: Showed 18 fields were blank 70% of time anyway

Result: Admin became advocate

Challenge 4: Cognitive Impairment

Problem: Some residents couldn't answer questions

Solution: Created observation-based checklist

Result: 100% completion rate

Key Takeaways for Healthcare Design

Clinical staff are overwhelmed - Designs must reduce burden, not add to it
Workflow is sacred - Tools must fit existing flow, not force new behaviors
Perfect is the enemy of done - Staff need "good enough" tools they'll actually use
Design for interruptions - Healthcare is chaotic, tools must accommodate that reality
Show, don't tell - Visual aids support memory and reduce cognitive load
Stakeholder buy-in requires stakeholder involvement - People support what they create
Measurement drives adoption - Data tells a compelling story to overcome resistance
